NeuroUX

Evaluating the first-impression of SmartTV GUI using EEG and eye-tracking

Project in collaboration with Samsung Electronics SSVD

Objective Development of a categorization algorithm that can evaluate the first impression of SmartTV interface based on viewer’s psychophysical response.
Timeline 2016.04 ~ 2017.3 (11 months)
Team & My Role

  • Project leader (1)

Background

Problem of Traditional UX Methods

Traditional UX evaluation methods that depend on user’s verbal reports or behavioral response may be easier to conduct in scale, but they hold the risk of holding secondary biases from factors such as social appropriateness, demand characteristics. More importantly, they rely on user’s recall minutes or hours after the experience which loses objectiveness and reliability.

Problem of SmartTV UX

Traditional UX methods can only evaluate the user’s experience in the long run usage of the smart TVs, whereas consumers’ purchase decisions are usually determined by short in-store experiences or by the first impression with the interface. Therefore, there needs to be a method that can evaluate the early consumer experience of smart TVs.

Solution

NeuroUX

We developed a NeuroUX method that can evaluate the viewer’s first viewing experience based on psychophysiological data such as EEG and eye-tracking. Unlike recall-based methods, psychophysiological responses can measure the instant response down to delay of (mili)seconds. It also enables objective evaluation of the user’s experience as users cannot consciously control brain signal, which could further be analyzed through eye-tracking on the elements that are attributable to change in experience. Such characteristics enable NeuroUX to be applied in in-store settings as the algorithm can instantly evaluate the consumer’s first impression of the SmartTV in a short period of time.

Method

Experiment Design & Model Development

We ran experiments where participants were presented with various SmartTV interface designs and were asked to passively observe the screen while we collected their EEG signals and gaze patterns. Then they were asked to give their subjective rating of the design based on their preference and presumed usability. Based on the selected EEG channels and frequency bands that have the highest correlation with user’s evaluation, we developed a model that can quantify user’s subjective experience. Eye-tracking data was used to analyze in fine-grain scale on how gaze-patterns differ between the initial observation of bad vs. good UIs.
